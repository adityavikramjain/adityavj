I Replaced PowerPoint with Code: The Evolution of an AI Presentation Engine

Most professionals dread making presentations. It's not because we lack ideas; it's because we are forcing our brains to do two contradictory things at once.

We try to be the Strategist (crafting a logical argument) and the Designer (aligning pixel-perfect boxes) simultaneously.

The result? We do neither well. We end up with "Frankendecks"—slides cluttered with walls of text, confused logic, and inconsistent fonts.

I wanted to solve this. Not by hiring an agency, but by treating presentations as a software engineering problem. Over six months and 20 iterations, I built Tandem Duo: a dual-engine AI system that separates "Thinking" from "Doing."

Here is how I engineered a system that forces me to think like the world's best presentation experts—and why I stopped using Google Slides entirely.

The Core Philosophy: Separation of Concerns

In software engineering, we have a principle called Separation of Concerns. You don't put your database logic in your user interface code. Yet, when we use standard AI tools (like ChatGPT) for presentations, we ask them to "Create a 10-slide pitch deck."

We are asking the AI to be the architect and the bricklayer at the same time. It inevitably fails, giving us generic bullet points on ugly slides.

To fix this, I split the system into two distinct agents:

  1. Maestro (The Brain): A ruthless strategist who doesn't care about fonts, only logic.
  2. Virtuoso (The Hands): A master craftsman who doesn't care about the story, only the pixels.

Phase 1: Maestro (The Strategy Engine)

The Problem: Early versions of my prompt (V6) acted like a "Yes Man." If I gave it garbage inputs, it politely organized that garbage into bullet points. It lacked the friction necessary for good strategy.

The Solution: I had to codify the wisdom of experts like Barbara Minto and Nancy Duarte directly into the system constraints.

1. The "Red Team" Protocol

In V21, I stopped asking Maestro to "write slides." Instead, I programmed it to run a Dynamic Interview. It now acts as a consultant using the SCQA Framework (Situation, Complication, Question, Answer).

  • Situation: What is the status quo?
  • Complication: Why is it broken now?
  • Question: What must we solve?
  • Answer: What is the single "Governed Thought"?

If I cannot answer these questions, Maestro refuses to generate the next step. It acts as a "Red Team," challenging my logic before a single slide is written.

2. The Narrative Sparkline

Nancy Duarte teaches that great presentations toggle between "What Is" (the status quo) and "What Could Be" (the solution). Maestro now enforces this tension. It ensures the narrative isn't just a list of features, but a journey from a problem to a resolution.

Phase 2: Virtuoso (The Execution Engine)

The Problem: Even with a great story, AI struggles with visual layout. My early attempts (V8) tried to make the AI "guess" where to put text. It resulted in broken layouts and overflowing text boxes.

The Solution: I realized that "design" in presentations isn't art; it's Cognitive Load Management. I turned to the principles of Garr Reynolds (Presentation Zen) and hard-coded them into CSS.

1. The Cognitive Load Calculator

I built a scoring system into the Virtuoso prompt. The AI analyzes the density of the content Maestro produces:

  • High Complexity: The system automatically triggers the .load-high CSS class, which increases white space, reduces font weight, and removes decorative elements.
  • Low Complexity: The system triggers .load-low, allowing for bolder typography and hero visuals.

This isn't an artistic choice; it is a mathematical rule that protects the audience from exhaustion.

2. The "No-Parse" Mandate

The biggest breakthrough came in Version 10. I discovered that if you let AI write JavaScript to manipulate the DOM, it breaks. It tries to get too clever.

I implemented the No-Parse Mandate: Virtuoso is forbidden from writing logic that touches innerHTML. Instead, it must generate a single, self-contained HTML file where all layout rules are handled strictly by CSS Grid and an Architectural Library of components (like .matrix-wrapper or .timeline-step).

The Result: Accessible Authority

By separating these concerns, the output changed dramatically.

  • Before (V6): I got a generic list of bullet points that I had to manually copy-paste into a slide template.
  • After (V21): I get a "Battle-Tested" strategy from Maestro, which flows into Virtuoso to generate a production-ready HTML file.

The system doesn't just make the work faster; it makes the work better. It forces me to answer the hard questions first. It effectively replaces the "Blank Page Panic" with a structured engineering process.

We often think creativity is about total freedom. But as I learned through 20 iterations of this tool, creativity thrives on constraints. By constraining the AI with the wisdom of Minto, Duarte, and Reynolds, we unlock its true potential: not as a generator of fluff, but as an architect of persuasion.
